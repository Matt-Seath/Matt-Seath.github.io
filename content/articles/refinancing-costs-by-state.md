Refinancing your home loan can save you thousands in interest, but it is not free. Understanding the full cost of switching helps you make a smart decision and avoid surprises.

## Typical Costs When Refinancing

Refinancing involves closing your existing loan and taking out a new one. You will encounter a mix of lender fees, legal costs, and government charges. Overall costs typically range from $500 to $2,000 or more, though fixed rate break costs can push this much higher.

### Common Refinancing Fees

| Fee | Typical Range | Who Charges It |
|---|---|---|
| Discharge (exit) fee | $150 to $700 | Your current lender |
| Application/establishment fee | $0 to $1,000+ (often waived) | New lender |
| Valuation fee | $0 to $600 | New lender or valuer |
| Settlement/legal fee | $100 to $1,000 | New lender or conveyancer |
| Mortgage registration fee | $115 to $250 per event | State/Territory government |
| Title search/admin | $50 to $100 | Lender or government |
| Break costs (fixed rate only) | Varies widely | Current lender |
| Lenders Mortgage Insurance | Varies | If new LVR exceeds 80% |

Many lenders waive application and valuation fees as an incentive for switching, and some offer cashback deals that can offset most or all of your upfront costs.

## Government Fees by State and Territory

When you refinance to a new lender, you generally pay two sets of government registration fees: one to remove the old mortgage (discharge) and one to register the new one. These are set by each state and territory.

### Mortgage Registration and Discharge Fees

Each state and territory sets its own fees for registering and discharging a mortgage. These typically range from around $100 to $250 per event, meaning you will pay two fees: one to remove the old mortgage and one to register the new one. The combined government fee for both is generally between $250 and $500 depending on your state.

These government fees are statutory and non negotiable, unlike lender fees, which can often be waived or reduced. Your conveyancer or broker can confirm the exact fees for your state.

### Total Refinancing Cost Estimate

To give you a clearer picture, total refinancing costs for a standard variable rate loan (with no break costs and a lender that waives application fees) typically range from $500 to $1,000 across most states once you factor in government fees, discharge processing, title searches, and settlement agent fees.

Actual costs will vary depending on your state, lender, and conveyancer.

## Cost Drivers That Can Make Refinancing More Expensive

### Break Costs on Fixed Rate Loans

If you exit a fixed rate loan before the fixed period ends, your current lender may charge a break fee to compensate for lost interest. This can range from a few hundred dollars to tens of thousands, depending on the remaining term and the difference between your fixed rate and current market rates.

**Example:** You fixed $400,000 at 5.99% for three years. After one year, comparable wholesale rates have dropped to 4.50%. The break cost formula considers the rate differential (1.49%), the remaining term (two years), and the loan balance. In this scenario, break costs could exceed $10,000.

Always ask your current lender for a break cost estimate before refinancing.

### Lenders Mortgage Insurance (LMI)

If your property has decreased in value (or you have increased your borrowing) and your new LVR exceeds 80%, you may need to pay LMI again. This is a significant cost and can make refinancing uneconomical in some cases.

## Does It Make Financial Sense to Refinance?

Refinancing makes sense when the savings outweigh the costs. Here is how to work it out.

### When Refinancing Does Make Sense

- You can secure a rate that is 0.20% to 0.50% (or more) lower than your current rate
- You will keep the loan long enough to recoup the upfront costs
- You need to change your loan structure (for example, from interest only to principal and interest, or to add an offset account)
- A cashback offer covers most or all of the switching costs

### When Refinancing Makes Less Sense

- The rate improvement is small relative to fees
- You are early in a fixed rate term and face large break costs
- You do not plan to keep the loan long enough to recover costs
- Your LVR has risen above 80%, triggering LMI

### The Breakeven Calculation

Here is a simple way to decide:

**Breakeven (months) = Total refinance costs / Monthly savings**

**Example on a $500,000 loan:**

| Scenario | Rate Reduction | Monthly Saving | Refinance Cost | Breakeven |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Small saving | 0.20% | ~$83 | $700 | ~8 months |
| Moderate saving | 0.40% | ~$167 | $700 | ~4 months |
| Strong saving | 0.60% | ~$250 | $700 | ~3 months |
| Strong saving (QLD) | 0.60% | ~$250 | $964 | ~4 months |

If you plan to keep the loan for longer than the breakeven period, the refinance is worth it.

### Should You Refinance Frequently?

Frequent refinancing simply to chase the lowest rate often is not worthwhile. The incremental savings can be eaten up by fees, unless you can heavily negotiate fees down or receive cashback offers that cover them.

A good rule of thumb is to review your loan annually and consider refinancing when:

- Your rate is noticeably above market
- Your circumstances have changed (higher income, more equity)
- Your current lender will not match competitive offers
- A compelling cashback deal is available
