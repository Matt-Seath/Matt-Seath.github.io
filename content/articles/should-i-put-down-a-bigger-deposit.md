You have been saving hard and have more than enough for a 20% deposit. Should you put down 25%, 30%, or even more? It sounds like a good problem to have, and it is, but the answer is not as straightforward as you might think.

## The Benefits of a Bigger Deposit

### Lower Repayments

The less you borrow, the lower your monthly repayments. On a $600,000 property at a current rate of 6.25% over 30 years:

| Deposit | Loan Amount | Monthly Repayment | Total Interest Over 30 Years |
|---|---|---|---|
| 10% ($60,000) | $540,000 + LMI | ~$3,392* | ~$681,000* |
| 20% ($120,000) | $480,000 | ~$2,955 | ~$583,800 |
| 25% ($150,000) | $450,000 | ~$2,770 | ~$547,300 |
| 30% ($180,000) | $420,000 | ~$2,586 | ~$510,800 |

*Note: The 10% deposit scenario includes Lenders Mortgage Insurance (LMI), which could add $8,000 to $15,000 to the loan amount depending on the lender and property value.

That is a difference of about $369 per month between a 20% and 30% deposit. Over the life of the loan, the smaller loan saves you approximately **$73,000 in total interest**.

### Potentially Better Rates

Some lenders offer sharper interest rates for borrowers with lower LVRs. You may unlock a better rate tier at 70% LVR compared to 80%. This rate improvement, even if it is only 0.10% to 0.20%, compounds the saving from a smaller loan balance.

## The Risks of Putting Everything In

### You May Need That Cash Later

Life is unpredictable. Once your deposit is locked into your property, you cannot easily access it. Consider what you might need funds for:

- **Emergency buffer**: 3 to 6 months of expenses is recommended as a minimum
- **Moving and settling in costs**: Furniture, repairs, utilities setup, and minor renovations can easily run to $5,000 to $20,000
- **Stamp duty and legal fees**: Make sure these are covered separately from your deposit
- **Property maintenance**: Unexpected repairs can be costly, especially on older properties
- **Other debts**: Higher interest debts (credit cards, personal loans) should typically be cleared first

### Opportunity Cost

That extra cash could potentially earn more working for you elsewhere:

- **Offset account**: Keeping extra funds in an offset account gives you the same interest savings as putting it in the deposit, but you can access it anytime
- **Investments**: If your expected return on investments exceeds your mortgage rate (after tax), investing the surplus may make more sense
- **Renovations**: Strategic improvements to a property can increase its value by more than the renovation cost

### You Are Locked In

Extra money in your deposit is equity you cannot touch without refinancing or selling. An offset account achieves the same interest reduction while keeping your money accessible.

## The Offset Account Alternative

This is often the best of both worlds. Instead of putting 30% down as a deposit, consider:

1. Put down 20% as your deposit (avoiding LMI)
2. Keep the extra 10% in a **full offset account**

You will pay the same amount of interest as if you had put down 30%, but you can access that money at any time for emergencies, opportunities, or unexpected costs.

### How an Offset Account Works

An offset account is a transaction account linked to your home loan. The balance in this account is "offset" against your loan balance when calculating interest. For example:

- **Loan balance**: $480,000
- **Offset account balance**: $60,000
- **Interest charged on**: $420,000 (the difference)

So $60,000 sitting in an offset account against a 6.25% home loan effectively earns you 6.25% tax free, because it is reducing the interest you pay. That is significantly better than most savings accounts, which pay interest that is then taxed at your marginal rate.

### The Numbers: Deposit vs Offset

Here is a direct comparison on a $600,000 property purchase:

| Strategy | Deposit | Loan Amount | Offset Balance | Interest Calculated On | Monthly Repayment |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Big deposit | $180,000 (30%) | $420,000 | $0 | $420,000 | ~$2,586 |
| Smaller deposit + offset | $120,000 (20%) | $480,000 | $60,000 | $420,000 | ~$2,955 |

Both strategies result in interest being calculated on $420,000. The difference is that with the offset strategy, your scheduled repayment is higher (because the loan is larger), but you have $60,000 in accessible cash. You can voluntarily direct extra funds toward the loan or keep them liquid for when you need them.

## When a Bigger Deposit Makes Sense

- You do not have an offset account available on your loan
- You want the certainty of lower fixed repayments
- You are an investor and want to maximise rental yield relative to repayments
- You already have a separate emergency fund and other investments
- You prefer simplicity and want the lowest possible loan balance from day one

## When You Should Keep Cash Aside

- You are buying your first home and may face unexpected costs
- You have access to an offset account
- You have higher interest debts that could be paid off first
- You want to keep cash available for renovations or improvements
- Your income is variable and you want a financial safety net
- You are considering buying an investment property in the near future
