Choosing between a fixed, variable, or split home loan rate is one of the biggest decisions you will make when taking out a mortgage. There is no universally right answer. It depends on your financial situation, your risk tolerance, and what you think rates might do in the future.

## How to Compare Current Rates

Home loan rates change regularly in response to the Reserve Bank of Australia's (RBA) cash rate decisions and competition between lenders. At any point in time, the rates available to you will depend on the lender, your loan to value ratio (LVR), and your loan size.

As a general rule, fixed rates for shorter terms (one to two years) tend to reflect where the market expects rates to head in the near term, while longer fixed terms (three to five years) build in more of a buffer. Variable rates move more directly with the RBA cash rate.

Your broker can confirm the current rates available for your specific situation.

## Variable Rate Loans

With a variable rate loan, your interest rate moves up and down with the market. When the RBA changes the cash rate, your lender typically adjusts your rate accordingly.

### Pros

- **Flexibility**: Most variable loans allow unlimited extra repayments with no penalty
- **Offset accounts**: You can link an offset account to reduce the interest you pay
- **Redraw facility**: Access extra repayments you have made if you need the funds later
- **No break costs**: You can refinance or pay off your loan at any time without penalty

### Cons

- **Uncertainty**: Your repayments can increase if rates rise
- **Budgeting difficulty**: Monthly repayments may change, making it harder to plan
- **Rate increases can be sudden**: Multiple rate hikes in a short period can significantly increase costs

### Best For

Borrowers who value flexibility, have an offset account with a healthy balance, or believe rates may fall in the near term.

## Fixed Rate Loans

A fixed rate loan locks in your interest rate for a set period, typically 1 to 5 years. Your repayments stay the same regardless of what happens to the cash rate.

### Pros

- **Certainty**: You know exactly what your repayments will be for the fixed period
- **Protection from rate rises**: If rates go up, your rate stays the same
- **Easier budgeting**: Consistent repayments make financial planning simpler

### Cons

- **Break costs**: If you want to refinance, sell, or pay off the loan early during the fixed period, you may face significant break costs (potentially thousands of dollars)
- **Limited extra repayments**: Most fixed loans cap extra repayments at $10,000 to $20,000 per year
- **No offset account**: Most fixed loans do not offer offset accounts (some lenders offer limited offset on fixed)
- **You miss out if rates fall**: If variable rates drop below your fixed rate, you are stuck paying more

### Best For

Borrowers who want predictability, are on a tight budget, or believe rates are likely to rise.

## Split Loans (Part Fixed, Part Variable)

A split loan divides your mortgage into two portions, one fixed and one variable. You choose the ratio (for example, 50/50, 60/40, or 70/30).

### Pros

- **Balanced approach**: You get some certainty from the fixed portion and flexibility from the variable portion
- **Partial offset benefit**: You can use an offset account on the variable portion
- **Reduced risk**: You are not fully exposed to rate movements in either direction
- **Some extra repayment flexibility**: Make unlimited extra repayments on the variable portion

### Cons

- **Complexity**: Managing two loan portions can be more complicated
- **Partial exposure**: You are still partially exposed to rate rises (on the variable portion) and partially locked in (on the fixed portion)
- **Break costs still apply**: If you refinance, you will face break costs on the fixed portion

### Best For

Borrowers who want a middle ground, some certainty and some flexibility, or who are unsure about the rate outlook.

## Key Factors to Consider

### 1. Where Do You Think Rates Are Heading?

Nobody can predict rates perfectly, but consider the current environment:

- If rates are high and expected to fall, variable may benefit you
- If rates are low and expected to rise, fixing may protect you
- If you are unsure, a split loan hedges your bets

### 2. How Long Will You Hold the Loan?

- Planning to sell or refinance within 1 to 2 years? Variable gives you flexibility without break costs
- Staying put for 3 to 5 years? Fixing could offer valuable certainty

### 3. Do You Have an Offset Account?

If you keep significant savings in an offset account, a variable or split loan lets you benefit from that. Fixing your entire loan means losing offset benefits.

### 4. Do You Make Extra Repayments?

If you regularly put extra money into your mortgage, variable is more accommodating. Fixed loans typically limit extra repayments.

### 5. What Is Your Risk Tolerance?

If the thought of your repayments increasing by $500 a month keeps you up at night, fixing some or all of your loan may give you peace of mind.

## A Practical Example

To illustrate, imagine two borrowers with a $500,000 loan over 30 years (principal and interest). One fixes their entire loan, and the other stays on a variable rate that is 0.40% higher.

That 0.40% difference works out to roughly $130 per month, or about $4,700 over three years. However, if the variable rate drops during that period, the variable borrower could end up paying less overall.

### What If Rates Move?

| Scenario | Variable Rate Outcome | Fixed Rate Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Rates drop over the fixed term | You save more on variable | You miss out on savings |
| Rates stay flat | Variable costs more if starting rate is higher | Fixed saves you money |
| Rates rise over the fixed term | Variable costs significantly more | Fixed protects you |

This is why there is no single right answer. It depends on your view of the future and your tolerance for uncertainty.
