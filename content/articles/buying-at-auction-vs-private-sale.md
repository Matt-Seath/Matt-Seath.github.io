When buying property in Australia, you will generally encounter two methods of sale: auction and private treaty (private sale). Each has its own process, advantages, and risks. Understanding both will help you feel confident no matter how the property you want is being sold.

## How Auctions Work

At an auction, buyers bid openly against each other on a set date. The property goes to the highest bidder once the reserve price (the seller's minimum acceptable price) is met.

Auction activity varies across Australian capital cities. In early 2026, national auction clearance rates have been hovering around 60% to 65%, with Sydney and Melbourne typically seeing the highest volumes. Brisbane clearance rates have been trending between 45% and 55%, reflecting a market that leans more heavily toward private treaty sales than the southern capitals.

### Key Features of Buying at Auction

- **No cooling off period**: Once the hammer falls, the sale is legally binding in most states
- **Deposit paid on the day**: Typically 10% of the purchase price
- **Transparent pricing**: You can see exactly what others are willing to pay
- **Set timeline**: The auction date creates urgency and a clear deadline
- **Finance must be arranged beforehand**: You need unconditional approval before bidding

### Tips for Auction Buyers

1. **Get pre-approved** with unconditional finance before the auction
2. **Set a firm budget** and do not get caught up in bidding wars
3. **Attend other auctions first** to observe and build confidence
4. **Arrange building and pest inspections** before auction day, as there is no time after
5. **Register to bid** on the day and bring valid identification

## How Private Sales Work

In a private sale, the property is listed at a set price (or price range). Buyers make offers to the seller's agent, and the seller decides whether to accept, reject, or counter offer.

### Key Features of Private Sales

- **Cooling off period**: Most states provide a cooling off period after signing the contract (see the table below)
- **Negotiation**: There is room to negotiate on price, settlement dates, and conditions
- **Conditions**: You can include conditions in your offer, such as "subject to finance" or "subject to building inspection"
- **More time**: The process is generally less rushed than an auction

### Tips for Private Sale Buyers

1. **Research comparable sales** in the area to make a competitive offer
2. **Include conditions** to protect yourself (finance, building inspection)
3. **Move quickly** if the property is popular, as other buyers may also be making offers
4. **Get pre-approval** so you can move with confidence
5. **Use your broker** to confirm your borrowing capacity before making an offer

## Cooling Off Periods by State and Territory

One of the biggest differences between auction and private sale is the cooling off period. After signing a contract in a private sale, most states give the buyer a window to withdraw (usually with a small penalty). At auction, there is no cooling off period at all.

| State / Territory | Cooling Off Period (Private Sale) | Penalty for Withdrawal | Auction Cooling Off |
|---|---|---|---|
| New South Wales | 5 business days | 0.25% of purchase price | None |
| Victoria | 3 business days | 0.2% of purchase price | None |
| Queensland | 5 business days | 0.25% of purchase price | None |
| South Australia | 2 business days | No set penalty (negotiable) | None |
| Western Australia | No statutory cooling off | N/A | None |
| Tasmania | No statutory cooling off | N/A | None |
| ACT | 5 business days | 0.25% of purchase price | None |
| Northern Territory | 4 business days | 0.2% of purchase price | None |

**Important**: The cooling off period can be waived in some states if the buyer obtains legal advice and signs a waiver. Always check current legislation and speak with your solicitor or conveyancer before signing anything.

## Which Method Is Better?

Neither method is inherently better. It depends on the market, the property, and your situation.

| Factor | Auction | Private Sale |
|---|---|---|
| Cooling off period | No | Yes (most states) |
| Conditions (e.g. subject to finance) | No | Yes |
| Price transparency | High | Lower |
| Negotiation | Limited | More flexible |
| Timeline pressure | Higher | Lower |
| Due diligence timing | Must complete before auction | Can be done during cooling off |
| Deposit | Usually 10% on the day | Negotiable (often 5 to 10%) |

**Consider auction if**: You are confident in your finance, have completed your inspections, and want a transparent process where you know you are competing fairly.

**Consider private sale if**: You want more flexibility, need conditions in your contract, or prefer a less pressured process with time to arrange finance and inspections.

## Getting Finance Ready

Whether you are buying at auction or by private sale, being finance ready is essential. Preapproval gives you a clear budget and shows sellers you are a serious buyer.

For auction purchases, pre-approval is not just helpful but essential. Because there is no cooling off period and no ability to include a "subject to finance" condition, you must have your finance locked in before you bid.

For private sales, pre-approval strengthens your negotiating position. Sellers and agents are more likely to take your offer seriously when they know your finance is sorted.
