Buying a home for your family is one of the most meaningful financial decisions you will make. Unlike buying your first apartment or investment property, a family home comes with a unique set of priorities: space, location, schools, safety, and room to grow. Here is how to approach it.

## What to Prioritise in a Family Home

### Location and Lifestyle

- **School catchments**: Research local schools and their catchment zones. This can significantly affect both your family's lifestyle and the property's value
- **Parks and playgrounds**: Outdoor spaces are essential for young families
- **Transport links**: Proximity to public transport, major roads, and your workplace
- **Safety**: Low crime areas with good street lighting and pedestrian friendly streets
- **Community**: Look for family friendly neighbourhoods with other young families nearby

### Space and Layout

- **Bedrooms**: Think about how many you need now and in 5 to 10 years
- **Living areas**: Multiple living zones are valuable as children grow and need their own space
- **Outdoor space**: A backyard, even a small one, adds significant value for families
- **Storage**: Families accumulate a lot. Built in wardrobes, a garage, and shed space matter
- **Layout flow**: Open plan living with sight lines to the backyard is ideal for supervising younger children

## Financial Considerations for Families

### Budgeting on a Family Income

If one parent plans to reduce their hours or take time off after having children, factor this into your borrowing calculation. Lenders assess your ability to repay based on your income at the time of application, but you need to make sure repayments remain comfortable if your household income drops.

A useful approach is to calculate your repayments based on one income only. If you can manage the mortgage on a single salary (even if tightly), you will have a strong safety net when one parent reduces their working hours.

### Parental Leave and Lending

Most lenders will consider an application from a borrower on parental leave, but their policies vary significantly. Here is what lenders typically want to see:

- **A return to work date**: Most lenders require this to be within 12 months, though some are more flexible
- **Confirmation letter from your employer**: Stating your position, salary, and return to work date
- **Evidence of leave payments**: Government Paid Parental Leave, employer paid leave, or both
- **Proof of ongoing employment**: Some lenders want a letter confirming your role will be available when you return

Some lenders will assess your application using your full pre leave salary as long as you have a confirmed return to work date. Others may only use a portion of your income or exclude parental leave pay entirely. Your broker can guide you to lenders with the most favourable parental leave policies, which can make a significant difference to your borrowing capacity.

### Using Family Guarantees

Parents or in laws can act as guarantors to help you buy sooner or buy a larger property. A family guarantee uses the equity in the guarantor's home as additional security, allowing you to borrow more or avoid LMI without a full 20% deposit.

This is a common arrangement for young families and, when structured correctly, can be released once you have built enough equity in your own home (typically when your loan to value ratio reaches 80%).

## Future Proofing Your Purchase

- **Buy slightly more space than you need today**: It is cheaper than moving again in a few years
- **Consider renovation potential**: A property with room to extend or reconfigure can grow with your family
- **Check council zoning**: Ensure there are no planned developments nearby that could affect your amenity
- **Think about resale**: Even if you plan to stay long term, buying in a desirable location protects your investment
- **Plan for childcare costs**: Factor in the cost of childcare alongside your mortgage repayments when budgeting. These are often the two largest household expenses for young families
