Interest rates are one of the most important factors in your home loan. Even a small difference in rate can mean tens of thousands of dollars over the life of your loan. Understanding how they work can help you make smarter borrowing decisions.

## How Home Loan Interest Works

When you borrow money to buy a home, you repay the principal (the amount borrowed) plus interest (the cost of borrowing). Most home loans in Australia charge interest daily and debit it monthly.

For example, on a $500,000 loan at 6.00% p.a. over 30 years:

- Monthly repayment: approximately $2,998
- Total interest paid: approximately $579,000
- Total amount repaid: approximately $1,079,000

That means you end up paying more in interest than the original amount you borrowed, which is why even a small rate reduction can make an enormous difference over time.

## The RBA Cash Rate and Why It Matters

The Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA) sets the official cash rate, which is the benchmark interest rate for the entire economy. When the RBA raises or lowers the cash rate, lenders typically adjust their variable home loan rates in the same direction.

However, lenders do not always pass on the full amount of a rate change, so it pays to keep an eye on what your lender is actually doing. You can check the current cash rate on the [RBA website](https://www.rba.gov.au).

As you can see, a 0.50% difference in rate on a $700,000 loan changes your monthly repayment by roughly $230. Over 30 years, that adds up to more than $80,000.

## What Influences the Rate You Get?

### Your Loan to Value Ratio (LVR)

The lower your LVR (meaning the more deposit you have), the lower your interest rate is likely to be. Borrowers with an LVR of 60% or less often qualify for the sharpest rates. Crossing the 80% LVR threshold also triggers Lenders Mortgage Insurance (LMI), which is an additional cost.

### Your Credit Score

A strong credit history demonstrates to lenders that you are a reliable borrower, which can help you secure a better rate. Defaults, late payments, and too many credit enquiries can all work against you.

### Loan Type and Features

Basic loans with fewer features often come with lower rates. Loans with offset accounts, redraw facilities, and other features may have slightly higher rates, but those features can save you more in the long run (see our article on [whether to chase the lowest rate](/articles/should-i-chase-the-lowest-rate/)).

### Loan Size

Some lenders offer rate discounts for larger loan amounts (sometimes called "premium" or "wealth" pricing). If you are borrowing above $500,000 or $750,000, it is worth asking what discounts are available.

## Fixed vs Variable: Which Is Right for You?

**Variable rates** move with the market. They offer flexibility (extra repayments, offset accounts) but your repayments can change when rates move.

**Fixed rates** are locked in for a set period (typically one to five years). They offer certainty, but may come with restrictions and break costs if you want to refinance early or make large extra repayments.

**Split loans** let you fix a portion and keep the rest variable, giving you a blend of certainty and flexibility. For example, you might fix 60% of your loan for three years and leave 40% on a variable rate with an offset account.

## Tips for Getting the Best Rate

1. **Save a larger deposit**: The more you put down, the better your rate. Aim for at least 20% to avoid LMI and access sharper pricing
2. **Clean up your credit**: Pay off debts and avoid late payments before applying. Check your credit report for errors
3. **Use a mortgage broker**: Brokers have access to a wide panel of lenders and can negotiate on your behalf. They often secure rates better than what you would get walking into a branch
4. **Do not just look at the rate**: Consider comparison rates, which include fees and charges, for a more accurate picture of the true cost
5. **Review regularly**: Rates change, and loyalty to your bank does not always pay off. Review your loan annually to make sure you are still on a competitive rate
