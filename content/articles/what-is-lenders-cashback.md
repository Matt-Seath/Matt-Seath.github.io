If you have been shopping around for a home loan, especially for a refinance, you have probably seen lenders advertising "cashback" offers. These promotions can put anywhere from $2,000 to $10,000 back in your pocket just for switching. Sounds great, but is it really free money?

## How Cashback Offers Work

A lender cashback offer is a one off payment made to you after you refinance your home loan (or sometimes take out a new loan) with that lender. It is essentially an incentive to win your business.

### Typical Terms

- **Amount**: Usually $2,000 to $5,000 for refinances, though some offers go higher
- **Payment timing**: Typically paid within 60 to 90 days of settlement
- **Minimum loan amount**: Most require a minimum loan size (for example, $250,000 or more)
- **New customers only**: Usually only available to borrowers switching from another lender
- **Time limited**: Cashback offers come and go as they are promotional, not permanent

### Examples of Typical Cashback Offers

Cashback amounts tend to vary by loan size and lender. Here is what has been typical in the Australian market:

| Loan Size | Typical Cashback Range |
|---|---|
| $250,000 to $499,999 | $2,000 to $3,000 |
| $500,000 to $749,999 | $3,000 to $4,000 |
| $750,000 to $999,999 | $4,000 to $5,000 |
| $1,000,000 and above | $5,000 to $10,000 |

These figures are indicative and change frequently. Some lenders offer flat amounts regardless of loan size, while others scale the cashback with the loan amount.

### How You Receive It

The cashback is usually deposited directly into your nominated bank account or your loan account after settlement. It is real cash and you can use it however you like.

## What to Watch Out For

### The Rate Still Matters Most

A $4,000 cashback sounds appealing, but if the loan's interest rate is 0.30% higher than a competitor without cashback, you could end up paying more over time.

**Example on a $500,000 loan:**

| | Lender A (With Cashback) | Lender B (No Cashback) |
|---|---|---|
| Interest rate | 6.19% | 5.89% |
| Monthly repayment | ~$3,062 | ~$2,963 |
| Monthly difference | +$99 more | Baseline |
| Cashback received | $4,000 | $0 |
| Extra interest over 1 year | ~$1,188 | $0 |
| Extra interest over 2 years | ~$2,376 | $0 |
| Extra interest over 3 years | ~$3,564 | $0 |
| **Net benefit at 3 years** | **+$436** | **$0** |
| **Net position at 4 years** | **Worse off by $752** | **$0** |

In this example, the cashback loan is slightly ahead for the first three years but falls behind after that. If you plan to hold the loan for five years or more, the lower rate loan wins by a wide margin.

Always compare the **total cost** of the loan (rate plus fees minus cashback) over the period you expect to hold it.

### Clawback Clauses

Some lenders include a clawback clause, meaning if you refinance away within a set period (usually 1 to 4 years), you may have to repay part or all of the cashback. Read the fine print carefully. Common clawback structures include:

- **Full clawback**: Repay 100% if you leave within 12 months
- **Sliding scale**: Repay a decreasing percentage over 2 to 4 years (for example, 100% in year one, 50% in year two, 0% after year two)

### It May Not Cover All Switching Costs

While cashback can offset refinancing costs (discharge fees, registration fees, and similar charges), it may not cover everything, especially if you are breaking a fixed rate loan.

### Tax Implications

For owner-occupiers, cashback is generally not considered taxable income. However, for investment property loans, the ATO may treat the cashback as assessable income. Speak to your accountant about your specific situation.

## When a Cashback Offer Is Worth It

- The loan itself is competitive (good rate, suitable features)
- The cashback more than covers your switching costs
- You plan to stay with the lender long enough that any clawback period passes
- You have compared the total cost against loans without cashback

## When to Be Cautious

- The rate is higher than alternatives, meaning the cashback is masking an expensive loan
- There is a long clawback period and you might want to refinance again soon
- The minimum loan amount is higher than what you need
- The loan lacks features you value (offset, extra repayments, and similar features)

## A Smart Way to Evaluate Cashback

Here is a practical approach:

1. **Ignore the cashback** and compare the loan on its merits (rate, fees, features)
2. If the loan is competitive on its own, the cashback is genuinely a bonus
3. If the loan is only attractive because of the cashback, be cautious
4. Calculate the **net benefit** over 2 to 3 years: cashback minus any extra interest you would pay compared to the cheapest alternative

### Quick Comparison Checklist

| Question | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| Is the rate competitive without the cashback? | Compare against lowest rates on the market |
| What is the clawback period? | Shorter is better; ensure it aligns with your plans |
| Does the loan have the features you need? | Offset account, extra repayments, redraw |
| What are the total switching costs? | Does the cashback fully cover them? |
| How long will you stay with this lender? | Cashback value diminishes if the rate is not competitive long term |

## Current Cashback Trends

Cashback offers tend to be more prevalent when lenders are competing aggressively for refinance customers. They are often available from mid tier and smaller lenders looking to grow their loan books, but the big four banks occasionally run them too.

In recent years, the trend has been toward lower cashback amounts (down from the $4,000 to $10,000 range common in 2022 to 2023) combined with more competitive rates. Some lenders have moved away from cashback altogether in favour of offering the lowest possible rate. Others continue to use cashback as a way to attract borrowers, particularly for larger loan amounts.

Offers change frequently, so what is available today may differ from next month.
