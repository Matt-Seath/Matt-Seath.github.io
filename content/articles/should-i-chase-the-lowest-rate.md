It is tempting to sort home loans by interest rate and pick the cheapest one. After all, a lower rate means lower repayments, right? While that is technically true, the lowest rate loan is not always the best loan for your situation.

## Why the Lowest Rate Can Be Misleading

A headline rate only tells part of the story. Some ultra low rate loans come with trade offs that can cost you more in the long run.

### Limited Features

The cheapest rate loans are often "basic" or "no frills" products. They may lack:

- **Offset accounts**: An offset account can save you thousands in interest over the life of your loan
- **Redraw facilities**: The ability to access extra repayments you have made
- **Extra repayment flexibility**: Some basic loans cap or penalise additional repayments

### Offset vs No Offset: A Real World Example

Let us compare two loans on a $600,000 mortgage over 30 years to see how features can outweigh a lower rate.

**Loan A**: 5.89% with a full offset account
**Loan B**: 5.69% with no offset account (basic loan)

At first glance, Loan B looks cheaper. The monthly repayment on Loan B is approximately $3,482, compared to $3,552 on Loan A, a saving of about $70 per month.

However, if the borrower keeps an average balance of $30,000 in the offset account attached to Loan A, the interest is calculated on $570,000 instead of $600,000. That $30,000 offset effectively reduces the interest charged each year by roughly $1,767.

Over 10 years, the offset account saves approximately $17,670 in interest, while the lower rate on Loan B only saves about $8,400 over the same period. The result: **Loan A with the offset saves more than $9,000 extra** despite having the higher headline rate.

If the borrower grows their offset balance over time (as many do with salary credits and savings), the advantage becomes even more dramatic. With $50,000 in the offset, the annual interest saving jumps to roughly $2,945, making the higher rate loan significantly cheaper overall.

### Hidden Fees

Some low rate loans make up the difference with higher fees:

- **Annual package fees**: $300 to $400 per year is common on packaged loans
- **Application or establishment fees**: Can range from $150 to $600 depending on the lender
- **Discharge fees**: Typically $150 to $400 when you eventually leave the loan
- **Valuation fees**: Some lenders charge $200 to $300 for property valuations

Always look at the **comparison rate**, which factors in most fees and gives you a more accurate picture of the true cost. By law, lenders must display the comparison rate alongside the headline rate.

### Restrictions That Cost You Later

Some low rate products lock you in with:

- Limited ability to switch between fixed and variable
- No ability to split your loan
- Restrictions on making lump sum payments
- Higher break costs if you refinance
- No portability if you want to move the loan to a new property

## What Should You Compare Instead?

### 1. Total Cost of the Loan

Look beyond the monthly repayment. Calculate the total interest plus fees over the period you expect to hold the loan. Most borrowers refinance or sell within seven to ten years, so modelling costs over that period is often more useful than looking at 30 year totals.

### 2. Features That Match Your Needs

If you have savings sitting in a transaction account, an offset account could save you far more than a 0.10% rate difference. If you receive irregular income (bonuses, commissions), flexible repayment options matter. If you are planning renovations, a redraw facility gives you access to extra repayments you have made.

### 3. Flexibility for the Future

Your needs will change. A loan that lets you switch between fixed and variable, increase your borrowing, or access equity gives you options down the track. The cheapest loan today may not be the best loan for where you are in five years.

### 4. Lender Service and Turnaround

A lender with fast approval times and responsive service can make a real difference, especially when you are buying at auction or in a competitive market. Some lenders can approve a loan in days while others take weeks.

## When the Lowest Rate Does Make Sense

Chasing the rate is more reasonable when:

- You are an investor focused purely on minimising costs and do not need an offset
- You have a straightforward loan with no need for extra features
- The rate difference is significant (0.5% or more lower)
- You have confirmed there are no hidden fees eating into the savings
- You plan to hold the loan for a long time, maximising the rate saving

## A Smarter Approach

Rather than chasing the absolute lowest rate, focus on finding the **best value loan** for your specific situation. That means weighing the rate against features, fees, flexibility, and your own financial habits. A loan that is 0.10% higher but comes with a full offset account and flexible repayments could save you tens of thousands more over the life of the loan.
