Owning an investment property can be a powerful wealth building strategy, but it comes with ongoing costs that need careful tracking. Keeping accurate records of your expenses is not just good practice. It is essential for claiming tax deductions and understanding your property's true performance.

## Common Investment Property Expenses

Here is a comprehensive list of the expenses most property investors encounter:

### Loan Related Costs

| Expense | Typical Range | Tax Deductible? |
|---|---|---|
| Interest on investment loan | Varies by loan | Yes |
| Loan establishment fees | $0 to $1,000 | Yes (spread over loan term or 5 years) |
| Ongoing loan fees | $0 to $400/year | Yes |
| Mortgage insurance (LMI) | Varies | Yes (spread over 5 years or loan term) |

### Property Management

| Expense | Typical Range | Tax Deductible? |
|---|---|---|
| Property management fees | 5 to 10% of rent | Yes |
| Letting fees | 1 to 2 weeks rent | Yes |
| Advertising for tenants | $100 to $500 | Yes |
| Lease preparation | $50 to $200 | Yes |

|---|---|---|

### Depreciation (Non Cash Deductions)

Depreciation is one of the most valuable deductions available to property investors because it does not require any out of pocket spending. You can claim depreciation on:

- **Building (capital works)**: For properties built after 1987, you can claim 2.5% of the original construction cost per year under Division 43 of the Income Tax Assessment Act. This deduction applies for up to 40 years from the date of construction completion.
- **Fixtures and fittings**: Items like carpets, blinds, hot water systems, and appliances depreciate over their effective life under Division 40. Note that since 2017, if you purchase a second hand residential property, you can only claim Division 40 depreciation on new assets you install yourself, not on existing fixtures.

A **quantity surveyor's depreciation schedule** (costing $400 to $800) can identify thousands of dollars in annual deductions you might otherwise miss. This cost is itself tax deductible.

### Other Commonly Overlooked Deductions

The ATO allows a range of additional deductions that investors sometimes forget to claim:

- **Accounting and tax agent fees** for preparing your investment property tax return
- **Legal expenses** related to tenant disputes or lease preparation
- **Stationery and postage** used for managing your property
- **Travel costs** for inspecting your property or collecting rent (note: travel to inspect prospective investment properties before purchase is not deductible)
- **Borrowing expense claims** such as title search fees, mortgage registration, and stamp duty on the loan

## What You Should Track

For each expense, record:

- **Date** of the expense
- **Amount** paid
- **Category** (interest, management, insurance, repairs, etc.)
- **Description** of what it was for
- **Receipt or invoice** (keep digital copies)

The ATO requires you to keep records for five years from the date you lodge your tax return, so organise your records in a way that makes retrieval easy.

## How to Organise Your Records

### Option 1: Spreadsheet

A simple spreadsheet works well for most investors. Set up columns for date, category, description, amount, and GST. Create a separate tab for each financial year and a summary tab that totals each category.

Key categories to track:

- Rental income received
- Loan interest
- Property management fees
- Insurance premiums
- Council and water rates
- Body corporate fees
- Repairs and maintenance
- Depreciation
- Legal and accounting fees

### Option 2: Property Investment Apps

Several apps are designed specifically for Australian property investors and can automate much of the tracking. They sync with bank accounts, categorise expenses, and generate tax ready reports. Popular options include property management portals offered by your managing agent, as well as standalone tools that integrate with the ATO's myDeductions feature.

### Option 3: Your Accountant

Many investors hand their records to their accountant at tax time. The better organised your records are, the less your accountant charges, and the less likely you are to miss deductions.

## Understanding Cashflow vs Tax Position

Your investment property has two financial pictures:

**Cashflow** = Rent received minus actual expenses paid

**Tax position** = Rent received minus all deductible expenses (including depreciation)

A property can be cashflow negative (costing you money each month) but still be a sound investment thanks to tax deductions, capital growth, and equity building. Understanding both figures helps you plan your budget and assess whether the property is performing as expected.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Not keeping receipts**: The ATO can disallow deductions without proof
- **Confusing repairs with improvements**: A repair (fixing a broken tap) is immediately deductible. An improvement (renovating a bathroom) must be depreciated over time as a capital works deduction
- **Missing depreciation claims**: Many investors leave thousands on the table by not getting a depreciation schedule
- **Not separating personal and investment finances**: Use a dedicated bank account for your investment property to make tracking straightforward
- **Claiming the wrong portion of expenses**: If your property is only rented for part of the year or is used partly for personal purposes, you can only claim the portion that relates to the rental period
