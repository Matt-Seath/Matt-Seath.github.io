Property investment remains one of the most popular wealth building strategies in Australia. But with so many approaches available, knowing where to start can feel overwhelming. Here are five strategies every beginner investor should understand.

## 1. Buy and Hold

The most straightforward strategy: purchase a property, rent it out, and hold it long term while it appreciates in value. This is ideal for investors who want steady rental income and long term capital growth without the complexity of active management.

Australian property has historically doubled in value roughly every 7 to 10 years in major capital cities, making buy and hold one of the most reliable paths to wealth creation.

**Best for**: Patient investors with a long term outlook (10+ years).

## 2. Negative Gearing

Negative gearing occurs when your rental income is less than your property expenses (loan interest, maintenance, management fees). The loss can be offset against your taxable income, reducing your overall tax bill.

While this strategy provides tax benefits, it is important to remember that you are still making a loss in the short term. The goal is for capital growth to outweigh those losses over time. Negative gearing works best for investors on higher marginal tax rates, where the tax offset is more valuable.

**Best for**: Higher income earners looking for tax advantages alongside capital growth.

## 3. Positive Cash Flow

The opposite of negative gearing: your rental income exceeds your expenses. These properties are harder to find in capital cities but can be more common in regional areas or high yield locations.

To give you a sense of what gross rental yields look like across Australia, here is a snapshot:

| City/Region | Typical Gross Yield (Houses) | Typical Gross Yield (Units) |
|---|---|---|
| Sydney | 2.5% to 3.5% | 3.5% to 4.5% |
| Melbourne | 2.8% to 3.5% | 4.0% to 5.0% |
| Brisbane | 3.5% to 4.5% | 4.5% to 5.5% |
| Perth | 3.5% to 4.5% | 4.5% to 5.5% |
| Adelaide | 3.5% to 4.5% | 4.5% to 5.5% |
| Hobart | 3.5% to 4.5% | 4.0% to 5.0% |
| Regional centres | 4.5% to 7.0%+ | 5.0% to 7.0%+ |

Higher yields are typically found in regional areas and outer suburban locations, while inner city properties tend to offer lower yields but stronger capital growth.

**Best for**: Investors who want their property to pay for itself from day one.

## 4. Renovate and Add Value

Purchase a property below market value, renovate it strategically, and increase both its rental yield and market value. This does not have to mean a full renovation. Sometimes cosmetic updates like new paint, flooring, and landscaping can make a significant difference.

A well planned cosmetic renovation of $20,000 to $40,000 can add $50,000 to $100,000 or more in value to the right property. The key is to focus on kitchens, bathrooms, and street appeal, which are the areas buyers and renters value most.

**Best for**: Hands on investors who can identify undervalued properties.

## 5. Using Equity to Grow Your Portfolio

Once your existing property has increased in value, you can use the built up equity as a deposit for your next investment. This allows you to grow your portfolio without saving an entirely new deposit from scratch.

Here is how the equity calculation works:

| Step | Calculation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Current property value | Market valuation | $850,000 |
| Amount still owing | Outstanding loan balance | $500,000 |
| Total equity | Value minus balance | $350,000 |
| Maximum borrowable (80% of value) | 80% x $850,000 | $680,000 |
| Usable equity | Maximum borrowable minus owing | $180,000 |

That $180,000 in usable equity could serve as the deposit and cover the purchase costs for an investment property worth up to approximately $700,000 to $800,000, depending on the lender and your borrowing capacity.

**Best for**: Existing homeowners looking to expand into investment property.

## Getting Your Finance Right

The right loan structure can make or break an investment property. Considerations include:

- **Interest only vs principal and interest**: Interest only loans keep repayments lower in the early years, which can improve cash flow and maximise tax deductions on your investment loan
- **Separate loans**: Keep your investment loan separate from your home loan for tax purposes. Mixing the two creates complications at tax time
- **Fixed vs variable**: Each has trade offs depending on your strategy. Fixed rates offer certainty, while variable rates provide flexibility and access to features like offset accounts
- **Offset accounts**: Can help reduce interest while maintaining access to funds. For investment properties, keeping your offset against your home loan (not your investment loan) is often more tax effective
