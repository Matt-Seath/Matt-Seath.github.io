One of the most common questions first home buyers ask is whether they should wait until they have saved a full 20% deposit before buying. The logic seems sound: a 20% deposit means you avoid paying Lenders Mortgage Insurance (LMI), which can cost thousands of dollars.

But waiting is not always the best strategy. Here is why.

## What Is LMI and How Much Does It Cost?

Lenders Mortgage Insurance protects the lender (not you) if you default on your loan. It is required when you borrow more than 80% of the property's value, meaning your deposit is less than 20%.

LMI costs depend on your loan amount and LVR (loan to value ratio). Here are approximate costs based on typical premiums from major LMI providers such as Helia (formerly Genworth) and QBE:

### LMI Costs for a $600,000 Property

| Deposit | Deposit Amount | LVR | Approximate LMI Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5% | $30,000 | 95% | $25,000 to $35,000 |
| 10% | $60,000 | 90% | $10,000 to $15,000 |
| 15% | $90,000 | 85% | $4,000 to $7,000 |
| 20% | $120,000 | 80% | $0 |

### LMI Costs at 90% LVR by Property Value

| Property Value | Loan Amount (90% LVR) | Approximate LMI |
|---|---|---|
| $500,000 | $450,000 | $8,000 to $12,000 |
| $600,000 | $540,000 | $10,000 to $15,000 |
| $700,000 | $630,000 | $12,000 to $18,000 |
| $800,000 | $720,000 | $15,000 to $22,000 |

LMI can often be added to the loan (capitalised), so you do not need to pay it upfront, but you will pay interest on it over time. For example, adding $12,000 of LMI to a $540,000 loan at 6% over 30 years adds approximately $26,000 in total repayments.

## The Case for Waiting

- **You avoid a significant cost**: LMI at 90% or higher LVR can add $10,000 to $35,000 to your loan
- **Lower repayments**: A smaller loan means lower monthly repayments
- **Better interest rates**: Some lenders offer sharper rates at 80% LVR or below
- **Stronger borrowing position**: More equity from day one

## The Case Against Waiting

### Property Prices May Rise Faster Than You Can Save

This is the biggest risk. If property values increase by 5% to 10% per year in your target area, your 20% deposit goal keeps moving further away.

### Worked Example: Saving vs Buying Now

Let us say you are targeting a property currently worth $600,000 and you have saved $60,000 (10% deposit). You can save $2,000 per month.

| | Buy Now (10% Deposit) | Wait and Save to 20% |
|---|---|---|
| Current savings | $60,000 | $60,000 |
| Target deposit | $60,000 (10%) | $120,000 (20%) |
| Time to reach target | Ready now | ~30 months |
| LMI cost | ~$12,000 | $0 |
| Property price at purchase (assuming 6% annual growth) | $600,000 | ~$696,000 |
| Deposit needed at purchase | $60,000 | ~$139,200 |
| Equity gained from price growth | ~$96,000 over 2.5 years | $0 (you do not own yet) |
| Rent paid while waiting ($550/week) | $0 (you own the home) | ~$71,500 |

In this scenario, the buyer who waits faces a moving target. The property grows by roughly $96,000 over 2.5 years, meaning the required 20% deposit jumps from $120,000 to approximately $139,200. Meanwhile, they have paid over $71,000 in rent that builds no equity.

Even after accounting for the $12,000 LMI cost, buying sooner can leave you significantly better off.

### The Cost of Rent While Waiting

Every month you spend renting is money that could be going toward your own mortgage. If you are paying $550 per week in rent, that is roughly $28,600 per year that builds zero equity.

### You Miss Out on Capital Growth

If the property you buy today increases in value, that capital growth is yours, even if you paid LMI to get in the door. Buying earlier means you start building wealth sooner.

### Government Incentives Have Time Limits

First home buyer grants, stamp duty concessions, and schemes like the Home Guarantee Scheme (which lets you buy with as little as 5% deposit with no LMI) may not be available forever.

## When It Makes Sense to Buy with Less Than 20%

- Property prices in your area are rising steadily
- You have stable income and can comfortably manage repayments
- You qualify for a government guarantee scheme (no LMI with 5% deposit)
- The LMI cost is small relative to expected capital growth
- You have been renting for a long time and want to start building equity

## When It Makes Sense to Wait

- You are close to 20% and can reach it within 6 to 12 months
- The property market in your area is flat or declining
- Your income is not stable enough to handle higher repayments
- You would be stretched too thin after paying LMI

## The Home Guarantee Scheme

The Australian Government's Home Guarantee Scheme allows eligible first home buyers to purchase with as little as 5% deposit without paying LMI. The government guarantees the difference between your deposit and the 20% threshold. The scheme includes several streams:

- **First Home Guarantee**: For first home buyers (5% deposit minimum)
- **Regional First Home Buyer Guarantee**: For buyers in regional areas
- **Family Home Guarantee**: For single parents and eligible single legal guardians (2% deposit minimum)
- **Price caps apply**: These vary by location and are updated periodically

Place allocations and property price caps are reviewed by the government each financial year, so check the [Housing Australia website](https://www.housingaustralia.gov.au) for the latest eligibility details. Places are limited, so it pays to act early.
